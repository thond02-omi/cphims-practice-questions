## Task 2.1 — Basic Clinical Vocabulary

> **[RE] RW-D2-11**: A physician orders "metformin 500 mg PO BID with meals." How many times per day should the patient take this medication?
> - A) Once daily
> - B) Twice daily **[CORRECT]**
> - C) Three times daily
> - D) Every 8 hours
>
> *Rationale*: BID = "bis in die" — twice daily. PO = by mouth (oral route). TID = three times daily; QID = four times daily.
> *Maps to: Task 2.1*

> **[RE] RW-D2-23**: A physician orders "morphine 2 mg IV STAT for pain." The term "STAT" instructs the nurse to administer the medication:
> - A) At bedtime
> - B) As needed when the patient requests it
> - C) Immediately **[CORRECT]**
> - D) Before the next scheduled meal
>
> *Rationale*: STAT (from Latin "statim") means immediately. PRN = as needed; QHS = at bedtime; AC = before meals.
> *Maps to: Task 2.1*

> **[RE] RW-D2-24**: A cardiologist orders "nitroglycerin 0.4 mg SL PRN chest pain." Which route does SL indicate?
> - A) Intravenous
> - B) Subcutaneous
> - C) Sublingual **[CORRECT]**
> - D) Inhalation
>
> *Rationale*: SL = sublingual (under the tongue). IV = intravenous; SQ/SubQ = subcutaneous; INH = inhalation.
> *Maps to: Task 2.1*

> **[AP] RW-D2-25**: A patient is scheduled for abdominal surgery at 7 AM. The pre-operative nursing checklist requires confirming the patient is NPO. What does NPO mean and why is it clinically significant before surgery?
> - A) No Prescription Orders — the patient should not receive medications
> - B) Nothing by mouth — prevents aspiration of stomach contents during anesthesia **[CORRECT]**
> - C) Non-Priority Orders — routine orders can be delayed
> - D) No Physical Output — urine output monitoring is temporarily suspended
>
> *Rationale*: NPO (nil per os — nothing by mouth) ensures the stomach is empty before general anesthesia to prevent aspiration pneumonia, a serious perioperative complication.
> *Maps to: Task 2.1*

> **[AP] RW-D2-13**: A 250-bed hospital reports 6,750 total inpatient patient days in September (30-day month). What is the Average Daily Census?
> - A) 250
> - B) 225 **[CORRECT]**
> - C) 6,750
> - D) 27
>
> *Rationale*: ADC = total patient days ÷ number of days in the period = 6,750 ÷ 30 = 225. Bed occupancy rate = 225/250 = 90%.
> *Maps to: Task 2.3*

> **[AN] RW-D2-14**: A hospital's Case Mix Index dropped from 1.85 to 1.62 after a specialty surgical center opened nearby and captured complex elective cases. The MOST likely financial consequence is:

> - C) Decreased per-case reimbursement because the remaining cases are clinically less complex **[CORRECT]**
> - D) Improved quality scores from reduced complexity
>
> *Rationale*: CMI reflects the average DRG weight. A lower CMI = lower complexity = lower reimbursement per discharge under DRG-based payment.
> *Maps to: Task 2.3*

> **[AP] RW-D2-28**: A 300-bed hospital has an Average Daily Census of 255. What is the bed occupancy rate?
> - A) 45%
> - B) 85% **[CORRECT]**
> - C) 115%
> - D) 300%
>
> *Rationale*: Bed occupancy rate = (ADC ÷ Licensed beds) × 100 = (255 ÷ 300) × 100 = 85%.
> *Maps to: Task 2.3*

> **[AP] RW-D2-29**: A hospitalist quality program tracks the percentage of patients who are readmitted within 30 days of a heart failure discharge. This metric is BEST described as:

> - C) Case Mix Index

> *Rationale*: 30-day readmission rate = (patients readmitted within 30 days ÷ total discharges) × 100. It is a key CMS quality and payment metric for heart failure.
> *Maps to: Task 2.3*

> **[AP] RW-D2-22**: During a BCMA audit, a nurse is found to have scanned the patient wristband but not the medication barcode, relying on visual identification of the medication package. Which of the Five Rights is MOST at risk?

> *Rationale*: Falls rate = (falls ÷ patient days) × 1,000 = (3 ÷ 600) × 1,000 = 5 per 1,000 patient days. This standard formula applies to falls, CLABSIs, CAUTIs, and other event-rate metrics.

## Task 2.6 — Additional CDS Questions (Set 2)

> **[RE] RW-D2-63**: A CDS alert fires because a patient's warfarin order may interact with newly prescribed aspirin, increasing bleeding risk. A separate alert fires because the patient has a sulfa allergy and was prescribed trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole. These are examples of:

> - D) Drug-allergy alert and drug-drug interaction alert, respectively

> - C) An early warning score / patient deterioration alert **[CORRECT]**
> - D) An evidence-based order set trigger

> *Rationale*: Early Warning Scores (NEWS, MEWS, SEWS) aggregate physiological parameters to detect patient deterioration risk — triggering escalation alerts before clinical collapse occurs.

> **[AP] RW-D2-65**: While a cardiologist is ordering a medication, a small icon appears next to the drug name. Clicking it opens a drug monograph and contraindication list from a clinical knowledge database — without leaving the ordering workflow. Which type of CDS intervention is this?

> **[AN] RW-D2-66**: A CDS alert fires when a nephrologist orders metformin 1,000 mg daily for a diabetic patient whose eGFR is 28 — metformin is contraindicated below eGFR 30 due to lactic acidosis risk. According to the Five Rights of CDS, this alert satisfies:

> **[AP] RW-D2-67**: A CDS alert correctly fires for a prescribing physician regarding a potential drug interaction.
